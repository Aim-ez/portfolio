The Opportunity

During my final year of university, I was invited to join Dr. Rashed Nekvi's research investigating Connected and Autonomous Vehicles (CAVs). The work was conducted in collaboration with the National Research Council of Canada (NRC) and focused on improving the safety and efficiency of autonomous vehicle platoons using the CARLA driving simulator.

The project culminated in a paper that was accepted for publication at the IEEE Vehicular Networking Conference (VNC) 2026, where I had the opportunity to present our findings in Porto, Portugal. You can read the paper on IEEE Explore at this link.

My Role

My work focused on designing and implementing the software used to simulate autonomous vehicles. I designed the experiment flow, automated the scripts that ran each experiment trial, and designed the scripts for vehicle spawning, data collection, traffic spawning, and environment setup. I developed new functionality within CARLA, implemented vehicle controllers, automated large batches of simulations, and analyzed the resulting data to evaluate different platoon configurations.

Technical Challenges

Building Platoons

CARLA didn't provide the platooning functionality our project required, so I implemented the logic needed within a Python script to coordinate multiple autonomous vehicles travelling together while maintaining safe following distances.

Working with CARLA

There really isn't anything as puzzle-pieced together as open-source research platforms. A large hurdle in this project was learning the simulation platform from scratch. Understanding CARLA's client-server architecture and extending an unfamiliar codebase required a lot of trial and error, debugging, persistence, and coffee.

Automation

Running experiements manually would have taken an enormous amoutn of time, so I developed scripts to automate simulations, restart experiements if an error occured, manage traffic generation, set environment variables, and collect results for later analysis.

Debugging Complex Systems

Many of the hardest problems came from unexpected behaviours within a complex simulation. From timing issues to memory leaks (that I may have thought broke my computer), solving problems often meant breaking them down one code block at a time.

Balancing Accuracy & Performance

One of the limitations of doing research at a smaller university is the processing power of your computer is limited to...the processing power of your personal machine. However, the simulation had to remain realistic while still running reliably on available hardware. Finding that balance meant carefully tuning parameters and making practical engineering decisions throughout the project.

Learning Through Research

Research rarely has a clear roadmap. This project taught me how to investigate unfamiliar problems, test different approaches, analyze results, and continually refine my work.

What I Learned

This project fundamentally changed how I approach software development. It taught me how to work with an existing codebase, design experiments, debug complex systems, and communicate technical findings through research. More importantly, it showed me that solving difficult problems often requires persistence, experimentation, and many iterations before arriving at a solution.
